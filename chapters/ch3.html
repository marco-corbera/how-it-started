Chapter 03 — 1993 to 2001

★ The Browser Wars & The Forum Era ★

Mosaic made the web visual. Netscape made it commercial. Internet Explorer made it a battlefield. And somewhere in the chaos of tiled backgrounds and Comic Sans, a parallel internet was forming — in forums, chat rooms, and bulletin boards where communities of strangers became something real.

Mosaic: The Web Gets Eyes

In January 1993, Marc Andreessen and Eric Bina released Mosaic at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications. It was the first browser that could display images inline with text — on the same page, not in a separate window.

The difference was immediately obvious. The web suddenly looked like something. Mosaic also ran on Windows and Mac, not just Unix workstations. Within a year, over a million downloads. The web had a face.

Curious fact

The original name for Mosaic was "X Mosaic" because it ran on the X Window System. The team considered calling it "Collage" before landing on Mosaic — a reference to how it could display text, images, and links all together on one page. Andreessen was 22 years old when he wrote it.

JavaScript: Born in 10 Days

In 1995, Netscape hired Brendan Eich. His assignment: create a scripting language for the browser. Time given: 10 days. The deadline was real — Netscape needed to ship Navigator 2.0 and JavaScript had to be in it.

What emerged was a language that could run directly in the browser and manipulate the page. For the first time, HTML pages could react to user actions. The 10-day origin story shows in the language's quirks — and there are many. But JavaScript's position inside the browser made it the most deployed programming language in history.

Curious fact

JavaScript was originally named "Mocha," then "LiveScript." It was renamed JavaScript purely as a marketing move to ride Java's popularity in 1995 — even though the two languages are completely unrelated. Same relationship as "car" and "carpet." This naming decision created confusion that persists 30 years later.

The Browser War

Microsoft bundled Internet Explorer free with Windows 95. If you had Windows, you had IE. Netscape charged for Navigator. The outcome was predictable — by 1998, IE had overtaken Netscape in market share. Netscape never recovered commercially.

But the competition produced chaos for developers. Both companies added proprietary HTML tags. Pages built for Netscape broke in IE and vice versa. "Best viewed in Netscape Navigator 4.0" was a badge of shame and pride simultaneously.

CSS appeared in 1996 to separate style from structure — the right idea — but browsers implemented it inconsistently for years. The cleanup would take a decade.

The Forum Era — Community Before Social Media

While the browser war raged, something quietly extraordinary was happening in the margins of the web: communities of strangers were forming around shared interests, and they were building cultures, friendships, and identities through text on a screen.

Usenet — The Original Forum

Usenet started in 1980, before the web existed. It was a distributed discussion system organized into newsgroups — each one dedicated to a topic. rec.arts.sf.written for science fiction. comp.lang.python for Python programmers. alt.fan.letterman for Letterman fans.

The alt.* hierarchy was the wild west — anyone could create a group, no approval required. This is where internet culture was born. Flame wars, trolling, spam, memes, drama — all of it originated in Usenet. Godwin's Law ("as an online discussion grows longer, the probability of a comparison involving Nazis approaches 1") was written in 1990, for Usenet.

IRC — Real-Time Before It Was Cool

IRC (Internet Relay Chat) launched in 1988. It was simple: you connected to a server, joined a channel (like #linux or #philosophy), and talked. Real time. With strangers across the world. In 1990, this was extraordinary.

IRC is where open source projects coordinated before GitHub. Where hackers organized. Where underground music scenes shared files before streaming. Where teenagers stayed up until 3am talking to people they'd never meet.

Discord is essentially IRC with better UX and a business model. Everything Discord does, IRC did first — but IRC looked like a terminal and required knowing what a server was.

phpBB and the Golden Age of Forums (2000–2010)

When the web arrived, bulletin boards moved with it. Early forum software like WWWBoard and UBB (Ultimate Bulletin Board) appeared in the late 90s. Then in 2000, phpBB 1.0 launched — free, open source, and easy to install.

Almost overnight, every community got its own forum. Gaming boards. Fan sites for TV shows. Regional meetup groups. Music scenes. Hobbyist communities for woodworking, aquariums, vintage cars, amateur astronomy.

Forum culture developed its own social fabric:

  • Post counts — your status in the community. "Newbie" vs "Senior Member"
  • Signatures — your personal brand: quotes, animated GIFs, motto
  • Rep points — karma systems before Reddit made them mainstream
  • Moderators — volunteers who enforced the rules, wielded by power of the ban hammer
  • In-jokes — references that accumulated over years, inaccessible to outsiders
  • OGs — the original members. Arrival date mattered.
Curious fact

GameFAQs — a gaming forum launched in 1995 — ran annual polls that became internet cultural events. "Best Video Game Character" bracket tournaments would crash servers and generate thousands of posts. Cloud Strife once beat Link in a final that the internet debated for months. These were the Super Bowls of early internet culture.

Why Forums Died

Forums peaked around 2005–2008. Then Facebook launched Groups. Then Twitter took real-time conversation. Then Reddit consolidated discussion with upvoting. Then Discord took gaming communities and then everything else.

Forums required you to go to a specific place. Social media put everything in one feed. Most people chose the feed.

What we lost: the depth. Forum discussions had history, search, threading. A great forum thread from 2004 is still readable and findable. A Twitter conversation from the same day is archaeologically irretrievable.

Some forums survived by going very deep on very specific topics — Stack Overflow (launched 2008) is essentially a Q&A forum that understood it was competing with a feed and won by being undeniably more useful.

Key Dates
Jan 1993

Mosaic released. First browser with inline images. The web stops being text-only. Marc Andreessen is 22.

Oct 13, 1994

Netscape Navigator 1.0 launches. Within months it commands over 75% of browser market share.

Dec 4, 1995

JavaScript ships in Netscape Navigator 2.0. Brendan Eich's 10-day prototype reaches the world. The browser is now a programming environment.

Aug 24, 1995

Windows 95 launches with Internet Explorer bundled. Lines around the block. The browser war begins in earnest.

Dec 17, 1996

CSS 1 published by the W3C. Style separated from structure — the right decision. Browsers will take years to implement it properly.

Jan 22, 1998

Netscape open-sources its browser — the code that becomes Firefox. A last act of defiance as Microsoft's IE reaches 50% market share.

Aug 1, 2000

phpBB 1.0 released. Free, open source forum software. Within years, tens of thousands of communities spring up. The forum era begins.

"The late-90s web was an aesthetic crime scene. Tiled backgrounds. Hit counters. Comic Sans. Animated GIFs. Under Construction banners. Guestbooks. The chaos of a medium that had no rules yet — and we loved it."
