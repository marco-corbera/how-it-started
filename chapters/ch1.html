Chapter 01 — 1960s to 1989

Before the Web

The internet is not the web. That distinction matters more than most people realize. Before Tim Berners-Lee wrote a single line of HTML, there was already a global network of computers talking to each other — and understanding how and why that network was built is the foundation for everything else in this story.

The Problem That Started Everything

It's 1962. The Cold War is at its peak. The United States military has a serious problem: its communication infrastructure is centralized. One nuclear strike on the right node and the entire network goes down. No communication, no coordination, no response.

The solution came from a researcher at RAND Corporation named Paul Baran. His idea was radical: what if communication didn't rely on a single path? What if data could be split into pieces, sent through multiple routes simultaneously, and reassembled at the destination? Even if part of the network was destroyed, the message would still get through.

This is called packet switching — and it's still how every message you send over the internet works today.

Core Concept

Packet Switching vs Circuit Switching

The old telephone system used circuit switching: a dedicated physical line was reserved between you and the person you were calling for the entire duration of the call. That line was yours, whether you were speaking or sitting in silence.

Packet switching works differently. Your data is broken into small chunks (packets). Each packet is labeled with its origin and destination, then sent independently through the network — each one potentially taking a different route. At the destination, they are reassembled in order. No dedicated line. No waste. If one path breaks, the packets find another.

ARPANET — The First Network

In 1969, the U.S. Department of Defense's Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) funded the first real implementation of these ideas: ARPANET. The goal was to connect research universities so scientists could share computing resources remotely.

On October 29, 1969, the first message was sent from UCLA to Stanford Research Institute. The system crashed after two letters. The intended message was "login". What got through was "lo".

Not a great start. But it worked in principle — and that was enough.

By 1971, ARPANET connected 15 nodes. By 1973, it had expanded internationally, reaching London and Norway. Email was invented almost as an afterthought during this period — Ray Tomlinson sent the first networked email in 1971 and chose the @ symbol to separate user names from host names. That choice is still with us.

What Is a Protocol?

Computers from different manufacturers, running different operating systems, needed to talk to each other. For that to work, they needed to agree on the rules of communication. Those rules are called protocols.

A protocol is just an agreement. When you send a letter in the physical world, there are implicit protocols: the address goes in a specific spot, the stamp goes in the corner, the envelope has a standard size. Without these agreements, the postal system falls apart.

Core Concept

What Is a Server? What Is a Client?

A server is a computer (or program) that waits for requests and responds to them. A client is a computer (or program) that makes requests.

Your browser is a client. It asks a server: "give me this page." The server responds: "here it is." That's it. The terminology sounds intimidating but the model is dead simple.

Servers don't have to be special hardware. Any computer can be a server. The difference is behavioral, not physical.

TCP/IP — The Language of the Internet

In the early 1970s, ARPANET researchers Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn designed the protocols that would eventually run the entire internet: TCP/IP.

IP (Internet Protocol) handles addressing. Every device on the internet gets an IP address — a unique identifier. Think of it as a mailing address for your computer. When a packet of data is sent, it's labeled with the sender's IP and the destination IP.

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) handles delivery. It breaks data into packets, sends them, and — crucially — confirms they all arrived and are in the right order. If a packet is lost, TCP requests it again. It guarantees delivery.

Together, TCP/IP became the universal language of networked computers. By 1983, ARPANET officially switched to TCP/IP. That date — January 1, 1983 — is often called the "birthday of the internet."

DNS — Why You Type Names, Not Numbers

Every device on the internet has an IP address: a number like 142.250.80.46. Memorizing those numbers for every website would be impossible.

The Domain Name System (DNS) was invented in 1983 to solve this. It's essentially a distributed phonebook that translates human-readable names (like google.com) into the IP addresses that computers actually use.

When you type a URL into your browser, before anything else happens, your computer asks a DNS server: "what is the IP address for this name?" That lookup happens in milliseconds. Only then does your browser know where to send the request.

Mental Model

How a Request Travels

You type example.com → DNS lookup converts it to an IP address → your browser opens a TCP connection to that IP → sends a request following HTTP rules → the server processes it and sends back a response → your browser renders it.

All of that happens in under a second. Every time you open a page.

What Existed Before the Web

By the late 1980s, the internet was real and functional — but it wasn't the web. What existed was a collection of services:

  • Email — person-to-person messages
  • FTP — file transfer between computers
  • Usenet — public discussion boards organized by topic
  • Telnet — remotely log into another computer's command line
  • Gopher — a menu-based system for browsing documents

These were useful. Scientists, academics, and military users loved them. But they weren't for everyone. They required technical knowledge and command-line familiarity. There was no way to link between documents. No images. No design. Just text and menus.

The internet was a tool. What it needed to become was a medium. That would take one person, at a physics lab in Switzerland, who had a specific problem to solve.

"The internet is not the web. The internet is the infrastructure. The web is what runs on top of it."
