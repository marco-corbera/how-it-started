Chapter 01 — 1960s to 1989

Before the Web

The internet is not the web. That distinction matters more than most people realize. Before Tim Berners-Lee wrote a single line of HTML, there was already a global network of computers talking to each other — and understanding how and why it was built is the foundation for everything else in this story.

The Problem That Started Everything

It's 1962. The Cold War is at its peak. The U.S. military has a critical problem: its communication infrastructure is centralized. One nuclear strike on the right switching station and the entire network goes dark. No communication, no coordination, no response.

The solution came from Paul Baran at RAND Corporation. His idea was radical: what if data could be broken into pieces, sent through multiple routes simultaneously, and reassembled at the destination? Even if half the network was destroyed, the message would still get through.

This is packet switching — and it's still how every message you send over the internet works today.

Core Concept

Packet Switching vs Circuit Switching

Old telephone networks used circuit switching: a dedicated physical line was reserved between you and the caller for the entire call — whether you were talking or sitting in silence. Wasteful and fragile.

Packet switching breaks data into labeled chunks (packets). Each packet travels independently, potentially via different routes. At the destination, they're reassembled in order. No dedicated line. If one route fails, packets find another.

ARPANET — The First Network

In 1969, ARPA (the U.S. Defense Department's Advanced Research Projects Agency) funded the first real implementation: ARPANET. The goal was connecting universities so scientists could share computing resources remotely.

The first message was sent from UCLA to Stanford. The intended word was "login." The system crashed after two letters. What got through was "lo."

Not a great start. But good enough.

Curious fact

The first email was sent in 1971 by Ray Tomlinson — to himself. He later admitted he couldn't remember what he wrote. He chose @ to separate usernames from host names because it was on the keyboard and rarely used in names. That symbol has now been sent trillions of times.

What Is a Protocol?

Computers from different manufacturers, running different operating systems, needed to communicate. For that to work, they needed agreed-upon rules. Those rules are called protocols.

A protocol is just a contract. When you mail a letter, there are implicit protocols: address format, stamp placement, envelope size. Without these agreements, the postal system collapses. Same idea — different medium.

Core Concept

What Is a Server? What Is a Client?

A server is a program that waits for requests and responds to them. A client is a program that makes requests.

Your browser is a client. It asks: "give me this page." The server responds: "here it is." Servers don't have to be special hardware. Any computer can be a server. The difference is behavioral, not physical.

TCP/IP — The Language of the Internet

In 1974, Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn published the paper describing TCP/IP — the protocols that would eventually become the universal language of networked computers.

IP (Internet Protocol) handles addressing. Every device gets an IP address. Think of it as a mailing address for your computer.

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) handles delivery. It breaks data into packets, sends them, confirms they all arrived, and requests retransmission of anything that was lost. It guarantees delivery.

On January 1, 1983, ARPANET officially switched to TCP/IP. That date is often called the birthday of the internet.

DNS — Why You Type Names, Not Numbers

Every device on the internet has an IP address: a number like 142.250.80.46. Memorizing those for every site would be impossible. DNS (Domain Name System), created in 1983, solves this — it's a distributed phonebook that translates google.com into the actual IP address computers use.

Mental Model

What Happens When You Type a URL

You type example.com → DNS lookup converts it to an IP → your browser opens a TCP connection to that IP → sends an HTTP request → server processes it and sends back HTML → your browser renders it.

All of that in under a second. Every single page load.

What Existed Before the Web

By the late 1980s, the internet was real and functional — but it wasn't the web. Services that existed:

  • Email — person-to-person messages, since 1971
  • FTP — file transfer between computers
  • Usenet — public discussion boards, the ancestor of every forum
  • Telnet — remotely log into another machine's command line
  • IRC (1988) — real-time text chat in channels. Discord before Discord.
  • Gopher (1991) — menu-based document browsing. Almost won.

These were useful. Scientists and academics loved them. But they weren't for everyone. There was no way to link between documents. No images. No design. Just text.

The internet was infrastructure. What it needed to become was a medium.

Key Dates
Oct 4, 1957

Sputnik launches. The U.S. creates ARPA in response. The agency that builds the internet begins as a reaction to losing the space race.

Oct 29, 1969

First ARPANET message: "lo". UCLA sends "login" to Stanford. The system crashes after two letters. The internet begins with a typo.

1971

First email sent by Ray Tomlinson. He picks @ as the separator and the symbol becomes immortal.

Jan 1, 1983

ARPANET switches to TCP/IP. Officially considered the birthday of the internet. All connected nodes had to upgrade at the same time.

1988

IRC invented by Jarkko Oikarinen in Finland. Real-time text communication in channels. The model Discord, Slack, and every modern chat tool is built on.

Nov 2, 1988

The Morris Worm — the first major internet worm infects ~6,000 computers (10% of the internet at the time). The age of cybersecurity begins.

"The internet is not the web. The internet is the infrastructure. The web is what runs on top of it."
